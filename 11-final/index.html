Nigeria is located in the West of the African continent, just above the equator. It is abounded on the north by Niger Republic, the east by Cameroon, the west by Benin, and the south by the Atlantic Ocean. Although many linguistic groups exist in Nigeria, there are three major tribes from which others can trace their origin - Igbo, Yoruba and Hausa. Each of these tribes have their own distinct language and are culturally different.

The Nigerian climate is warm and humid, and consists of two major seasons year round – the rainy and the dry seasons. Rainy season lasts approximately from February to October (typically in the Southeastern and coastal parts of the country). The dry season runs from November to about January, and is a predominate season in the Northern regions.

Successful architecture is one that highlights harmony between the content (the building) and the context (the natural landscape). Here in Igboland, its natural landscape is beautiful and begs that any additions only enhance and not take away from this fact. While understanding that big houses accomodate more people and possessions, the truth is building “big” is not without negative consequences. These include the obvious increase in construction costs, time, labor, and the level of difficulty.

The “mc-mansion” houses springing up in today’s Nigerian village (specifically Igbo villages) sometimes ignores this. In order to build “big” huge portions of the natural vegetation are razed and the yard areas paved over. Some compounds do have plantings but they are dwarfed by the scale of the house, in the end it becomes a fight between content (the building) and the context (the natural landscape).

The end goal of my Thesis research and study is to obtain a residential architectural vernacular within the Igbo village that: 1. Speaks to this idea of “CONTENT + CONTEXT” 2. Maintains a sense of “PLACE” unchanged even when the “TIME” does.

Design Principles

To this effect, a set of DESIGN PRINCIPLES intended to guide the future village home builder is proposed. The following nine points further expand on and explain the above mentioned goals:

  • Style and Form
  • Scale and Siting
  • Space Composition
  • Space and Function
  • Building Materials
  • Material Composition
  • Vegetation
  • Energy Conservation
  • Climate Considerations

The architectural style of the house should be respectful and representative of its surrounding, i.e. 20 ft high Romanesque columns do not belong on a Nigerian house, especially within the village setting.

Multi-storied houses do not necessarily have to equal “humungus”. The scale of the house should be proportional to the size of the site, and because it is residential, it should also be relative to human scale.

The exterior shape/form of the house generally affects its interior layout.Therefore, the exterior should balance out the interior in terms of space usage. Most of these monumental houses sometimes have leftover space at the interior (especially at the first floor) with no intentional programing. The most common example of this is houses that end up with oversized living rooms due to a huge footprint; it makes the space difficult to furnish and unnecessarily monumental in width and height.

Some spaces within the house within the house are most useful when they are flexible and made adaptable to multiple functions . An “open plan” arrangement at the first floor allows for this (especially areas designated as public domain) will benefit from this. Two spaces can be separate or shared through the use of movable partitions; e.g. Living and Family Room.

Materials for building in these parts of Nigeria are readily available. Use building materials that: 1. Embody the culture and, 2. Represent appropriate technological advancements of the “time and place”; i.e. mud bricks (if one-storey high), clay tiles, concrete, CMU, bamboo, timber and palm fronds. Use some metals; floor mats; and fabric that are representative of the tribe. It is important to preserve the essence of the village architecture, regardless of technological advancements; the significant elements of the village and village life can remain intact.

Maintain historic design and building practices by using elements that enhances the uniqueness of the village. A healthy balance between “old” and “new” design elements and principles can be created and high-lighted in the houses being built in today’s village. Combinations such as loam walls (wattle and daub construction), thatched roof, clay tile roof and walls, wood carved doors, exterior walls with decorative etchings, vegetation and the use of low mud wall surrounds to divide compounds will produce a house that showcases an essence of the village.

The natural vegetation existing throughout the Southern regions offers not just building resources, but beauty and sustenance; they should be used in the overall compound layout. Plants and trees are simple but great elements that can enhance one’s house . Vegetation such as Coconut trees, Orange trees, Hibiscus plants, Palm trees, Mahogany and Mango trees, are some of the most common indigenous vegetation found in this tropical

Use building materials and energy conserving practices to create a cool and healthy living environment. This is not a foreign concept in the Nigerian architecture as research shows that this was the typical practice. Although the culture celebrates the extended family and providing separate living quarters for different family sects, it is prudent to build one main house for living in lieu of the former “big house + multiple smaller ones” within one compound. This will keep costs and material usage to a minimum. Natural ventilation practices should be implemented in order to eliminate the dependency on air conditioning. These include plantings close to the house; the use of building materials such as masonry at the walls and roofs; and the use of casement windows to allow direct airflow/exchange. In lieu of air conditioning units, ceiling fans are great for air circulation and cooling (especially when the space it is cooling is of a small volume, e.g. any areas of 250 square feet and below). As Nigeria is a tropical climate, energy from the sun can be easily harnessed through photovoltaic technology.

The Nigerian village, as well as most countries considered as third-world could be seen as places where sustainable and “green” practices are implemented. Houses were constructed with thatched roofs made from palm fronds and mud bricks. These supported natural ventilation and air flow, and kept spaces within the house cool during harsh heat and humidity. Covered porches and verandahs were built and used for outdoor seating and gathering, while acting as a buffer between the house and the climate. Also the use of large roof overhangs protected the building itself from some damge from rainfall and UV rays from the sun.
